Technical writing

First Isomorphism Theorem

A group is a set with an operating which follows certain rules. There are certain mappings between groups that preserve the operation; these mappings are called (group) homomorphisms.

There is a special type of homomorphism called an isomorphism. An isomorphism between two groups basically allows you to claim that the two groups are the same. You can think of it as an equal sign just for groups. (In fact, like the notion of numeric equality on numbers, isomorphisms form an equivalence relation on groups.) When we have two groups G , H which are isomorphic to each other, we write G ≃ H .

One of the most important and basic theorems of abstract algebra is the First Isomorphism Theorem (there are three, laid out by mathematician Emmy Noether in the 1920s). The First Isomorphism Theorem states that, given two groups G , H and a homomorphism ϕ : G → H the image of ϕ is isomorphic to the quotient group G / ker ϕ .

Q: Wait, what's a quotient group? I won't define quotient group formally but the idea of a quotient is very fundamental to any kind of algebraic structure. I once had a math professor say that a lot of higher-level math is just about turning things to zero, and this is basically what taking the quotient of things is; asking what if this was zero? How would this affect the idea of equivalence between two elements? This is a reasonable thing to ask because if we let non-zero things act like they were zero, suddenly two things that differ by this non-zero thing are now equal!

Think of ker ϕ as all the stuff that is collapsed by ϕ to zero. In other words, ϕ sort of destroys some part of G ; since it's a homomorphism and not an isomorphism, it can potentially destroy some information about G when everything in it is mapped to H .

Meanwhile, the image of ϕ is what you get when you apply ϕ to everything in G .

So, the First Isomorphism Theorem says that the group H you get when you apply ϕ to everything in G is in some sense equal to the original group G hat you started with, but only if you mod out what gets collapsed by ϕ (hence G / ker ϕ and not just G ). This makes intuitive sense because \phi leaves behind some information about G, so H doesn't necessarily equal G anymore. Modding out by \ker \ph is precisely what handles this possible discrepancy between G and H.

In a sense the size of ker ϕ is a measure of how much ϕ preserves of the original group. Then it makes sense that for any isomorphism f : G → H , ker f = 0 . We also say that ker f is trivial. I'm being very vague with notation here, but this means that ker f is the smallest it can possibly be, therefore f preserves as much as G as possible when mapping to H , therefore G is equal to H , i.e., nothing is lost/collapsed when we apply f to G .

My favorite homomorphism and its fibres

An upperclassman I know gave once gave me and a group of other students brilliant lecture on the deep relationship between congruence classes, partitions, and fibres. When I first came across this concept in Artin's Algebra I remember being elated. (Every time I consider giving up I think about these concentric circles on the plane. It's an image I could put in my wallet as a reservoir of spiritual strength, like an illustration of a saint... is it a coincidence that these circles resemble haloes...?)

A fibre is our best attempt at an inverse mapping. Not all maps have an inverse map, particularly it's impossible to construct an inverse of this map:

because while we can easily go in one direction, it's not entirely clear on how to proceed in the other.

However, it still might be valuable to talk about a relationship that goes in that other direction. Fibres are a generalization which allows us to to talk about pre-images without the requirement that the map be one-to-one and onto. You might recognize that a the kernel of a homomorphism is a type of fibre.

(NB: fibres and pre-images are the same thing.)

More formally, given two sets A,B and a mapping \psi : A \rightarrow B, the fibre is a subset of A. Specifically, given any element b ∈ Im ψ (here note that Im ψ ⊂ B ), the fibre of b is written ψ − 1 ( b ) (but should not be conflated with the inverse) where ψ − 1 ( b ) ⊂ A defined like so: ψ − 1 ( b ) = { a ∈ A ∣ ψ ( a ) = b } . In words, it's all the elements in A which map to the specific b in B.

All group homomorphisms induce a partitioning on the original group, via fibres. A partition is a way to split a set into chunks such that (1) no element is left out and (2) there are no overlaps between the chunks. Given our definition of fibres, can you see why they must form a partition? (It might seem trivial to some of you, but I found it quite fun to thoroughly convince myself of this.)

A: Let's focus on condition (2) and proceed by contradiction. Suppose we had two fibres of some homomorphism g : G \rightarrow H. Then suppose a, b \in H and a \ne b, and consider the fibres g^{-1}(a) and g^{-1}(b). Each of these are subsets of G. Suppose by contradiction that an element g \in G is in both g^{-1}(a) and g^{-1}(b) (i.e., g is in their intersection g^{-1}(a) \cap g^{-1}(b)). By definition that means \phi(g) = a and \phi(g) = b. But this is impossible; an element of G can map to at most one in H. Thus the intersection g^{-1}(a) \cap g^{-1}(b) is empty. In other words, no fibres overlap.

Congruence classes are a special, group-specific instance of the more general equivalence classes for sets. It can easily be shown that equivalence classes (and therefore congruence classes) of a set/group form a partition. We won't need congruence classes for what I'm saying next so I'll just throw their name out there and move on.

All of this was a setup so that I could talk about my favorite example of fibres of a homomorphism. (This also ties into the First Isomorphism Theorem and I find it quite beautiful.)

There is a homomorphism from the complex plane \mathbb C to the non-negative real line \mathbb R^{\ge 0}. You might be able to guess what it is; it's the modulus/norm of the complex number a + bi, which is \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}. Can you see why it isn't an isomorphism? (Hint: the complex plane is two-dimensional whereas the real line is one-dimensional (we're going from a plane to a line, so surely the map must be lossy).

What does the kernel of this homomorphism look like? (What points of the complex plane get mapped to zero on the non-negative real line?)

A:

These are the points of form (a, b) on the complex plane such that \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} = 0, which is only possible if a = 0 and b = 0. So, only the point (0,0), i.e., the origin, is in the kernel. You can also reason through this by noting that the origin itself is the only point in the plane with distance from the origin being zero.

What do the fibres of this homomorphism look like?

A:

Let's start small. Consider the fibre g^{-1}(1). These are all the points in the complex plane that get mapped to 1 on the real line. By definition these are all the points of form (a,b) such that \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} = 1. You might recognize this as the equation of a circle with radius 1, and in fact this is precisely what g^{-1}(1) looks like.

Generalize to g^{-1}(\alpha) where \alpha can be any real number. So the fibres are circles around the origin of radius \alpha. It would be impossible to draw all the fibres of g because they're an uncountable number of circles in the complex plane, but here's a drawing of a few fibres to get the gist:

And one doesn't even need the First Isomorphism Theorem to see that these circles feel very similar to the non-negative real line, despite being in the complex plane. The theorem allows us to formally state that this indeed is the case. To see this more clearly, note that the circles only differ by their radius. The length of the radius can be expressed by any single non-negative real number. Every circle is then just a point on the non-negative real line; this is where the isomorphism lies.

Also, we decided to make a joint-effort post on interesting mathematical words in our respective mother-languages. Sonya has a list of Russian words here. My list in Korean is as follows:

Math in Korean is 수학 (数学; su-hak, compare with Japanese suugaku) which comes from the Chinese characters for number and knowledge/teaching. You will see this syllable, 수 (数), come up a lot. A lot of academic words in Korean are taken straight from Chinese and so have many analogs in both Chinese and Japanese, which is why I include the characters.

The astute among you may ask: what's the reasoning behind your romanizations of Korean? It doesn't look like Yale or McCune–Reischauer... yeah, that's right. I hate those two with a passion. I propose my own completely nonstandardized and unorthodox method. Good luck trying to read it.

  • 유리수 (有理数; yu-ri-su): rational numbers - Since Korean has a limited phonology, there are many homophones and amusing coincidences which arise from said homophones. The characters for rational numbers are exist, logic/reason, and number, which makes sense! Being rational means being reasonable... but the word for glass is also 유리 (yu-ri), so when I think of rational numbers I always envision greenish old-fashioned Coke bottles...
    • Accordingly, irrational numbers are numbers without logic: 무리수 (無理数; mu-ri-su), and integers are literally numbers that are nicely arranged/ordered, 정수 (整数; jung-su). Funnily enough, imaginary numbers are literally lying numbers: 허수 (虛数; heo-su)
      • .
  • 음수 (陰数; eum-su) and 양수 (陽数; yang-su): negative and positive numbers - Are literally yin-numbers and yang-numbers.
  • 핵 (核; haek): kernel - The character here means all sorts of things, from the pit/seed of a fruit (great mental image; the kernel of a homomorphism being a peach pit), to nucleus (of a cell or atom), to nuclear weapons (!). Very evocative character, and one that I usually see/hear in the context of nuclear power.
  • 동형 사상 (同形 写像; dong-hyeong-sa-sang): isomorphism - From the characters for same, shape, copy/describe/photograph, and image.
  • 정리 (定理; jung-ri): theorem - See the second character, which means logic. Homophonic with the word for cleaning up, which is a nice coincidence. Very much evokes tying up loose ends.
  • 보조정리 (補助定理; bo-jo-jung-ri): lemma - 보조 (補助; bo-jo) literally meaning assistant. A lemma is an assistant theorem.
  • 함수 (箱数; ham-su): function - Is just a box of numbers. 함 (箱; ham) brings to mind a treasure chest or a safebox. Coincidentally this isn't so far off from how functions are usually taught to kids: boxes that take inputs and produce certain outputs.
